PROJECT OVERVIEW

THE REVOLUTION CHANGES HISTORY

Main Message: The French Revolution changed France politically and socially by challenging unfair systems and spreading ideas of liberty, equality, and democracy. However, the revolution also caused violence and instability, showing that not all of its goals were fully achieved.

What was the French Revolution?

The French Revolution can be defined as a period during which the common citizens of France rebelled against the oppression they were facing, as well as against the absolute rule of the monarchy. Commoners faced many issues such as poverty, high taxes, and a lack of political rights, while the nobility enjoyed comfort and luxury in France.

Why was it important?

The French Revolution was important because it ended the absolute monarchy, reduced the power of the nobility, and gave people more rights and freedoms. Historians still study it today because it changed politics, inspired other revolutions around the world, and showed how people can fight for equality and justice. It changed France by creating a republic, spreading ideas of liberty and equality, and changing the social system so people were treated more equally under the law.

Who was most affected?

The people who were most affected were the Third Estate, particularly peasants and laborers, because before the French Revolution they were subjected to excessive taxation, hunger, and poverty. The monarchy and nobility were also affected, since many of their powers and privileges were taken away from them.

GOALS OF THE REVOLUTION

LIBERTY AND EQUALITY

There were many goals that influenced people to start the French Revolution. One of the most important goals was replacing the old system, which was an unfair system that supported the upper classes while ordinary people had very few rights. The people also wanted freedom of speech so they could express their opinions and thoughts without being controlled by the government. Another major goal was achieving a representative government that allowed citizens to choose representatives to help create laws and make decisions for the country.

They also aimed to gain equal representation so that ordinary citizens would have the same political power as the nobles and clergy. In addition, the people of France wanted to abolish the noble veto because even though the Third Estate had the largest population, it still had only one vote, which made the system unfair. Overall, the people wanted judicial reform and a fairer legal system where laws and punishments applied equally to everyone instead of favoring the rich and discriminating against the poor.

CAUSES OF THE REVOLUTION

CAUSES OF THE REVOLUTION

Causes

Social Inequality, Economic Problems, and Enlightenment Ideas

The French Revolution started because of social inequality, economic problems, and Enlightenment ideas. France was divided into the Three Estates: the First Estate (clergy), the Second Estate (nobility), and the Third Estate (common people). The Third Estate made up about 98% of the population but paid most of the taxes, while the First and Second Estates had privileges. France also had huge debt from wars and high food prices caused by poor harvests, which made people angry. Enlightenment thinkers like Jean-Jacques Rousseau inspired people to demand liberty and equality. These problems pushed the people toward revolution.

KEY EVENTS

THE ESTATES GENERAL

May 1789

The Estates General

The Estates General was a meeting called by Louis XVI in May 1789 to solve France's financial crisis. It brought together representatives from the three estates. The Third Estate wanted fair voting because they represented most of the population, but each estate only had one vote. This meant the clergy and nobility could outvote them. The unfair system caused anger and led the Third Estate to break away and form the National Assembly, which became a major turning point in the revolution.

The Estates General Meeting

THE TENNIS COURT OATH

The Tennis Court Oath

June 20, 1789

The Tennis Court Oath

The Tennis Court Oath was a moment when members of the Third Estate got locked out of their meeting. So, they went to a tennis court instead, saying they would not leave until France had a constitution. This move showed they were willing to challenge the king’s power and that they wanted change in France. The Tennis Court Oath was a step where the Third Estate showed they were serious about creating a fair and equal constitution.

THE STORMING OF THE BASTILLE

July 14, 1789

Storming of the Bastille

The Storming of the Bastille occurred when people in Paris attacked the Bastille prison to get weapons and gunpowder. The Bastille was a symbol of the king’s power and control. When it fell, it became a strong symbol of resistance against the king and his rule. This event is still seen today as the start of the French Revolution.

Storming of the Bastille

DECLARATION OF THE RIGHTS OF MAN

Declaration of the Rights of Man

Summer 1789

Declaration of the Rights of Man

The Declaration of the Rights of Man was created in the summer of 1789. It was created by the French National Assembly, and it was made to create basic rules and rights for a new government in France. It was a response to the old French monarchy, where rich people had special privileges and ordinary citizens did not have equal rights.

One important person connected to this document was Marquis de Lafayette, who helped draft the Declaration of the Rights of Man with support from Thomas Jefferson and presented it to the National Assembly. Marquis de Lafayette supported ideas such as religious freedom and ending the slave trade. The Declaration was also inspired by Enlightenment thinkers who believed in equality, freedom, and natural rights. The Declaration changed France by introducing ideas of freedom, equality, and individual rights, and it helped create the foundation of a more democratic French government.

THE ROYAL EXECUTIONS

January 21 and October 16, 1793

Executions of Louis XVI and Marie Antoinette

During the French Revolution, Louis XVI and Marie Antoinette were executed by guillotine after being accused of betraying France. Louis XVI was executed on January 21, 1793, because people believed he was working against the country and trying to keep his power as king. Marie Antoinette was put on trial later, and she was accused of helping France’s enemies and wasting money while the people were suffering and starving. She was executed on October 16, 1793. Their deaths ended the French monarchy.

ROLE OF WOMEN

WOMEN IN THE REVOLUTION

Women's March on Versailles and Women's Rights

Despite the fact that women had minimal rights at the time of the French Revolution, many participated in protests demanding better living conditions due to worsening poverty and high food prices. The biggest and most famous action taken by women was the Women's March on Versailles in 1789, when thousands of women protested for bread and forced the king to listen to his citizens' complaints. Thus, it is evident that ordinary women played an important role in political events during the revolution.

Furthermore, women established political clubs and printed pamphlets regarding equality and women’s rights. One such document was written by Olympe de Gouges, who argued that both genders should have equal rights in her Declaration of the Rights of Woman and the Female Citizen. Nevertheless, although women actively engaged in the struggle, many of their demands were ignored afterward.

Analysis: However, despite the revolution promoting liberty and equality, women were still excluded from many political rights. Their participation remained significant because it helped spread revolutionary ideas and inspired future movements for women’s rights.

THE REIGN OF TERROR

THE REIGN OF TERROR

1793–1794

The Reign of Terror

One of the most extreme phases of the French Revolution was the Reign of Terror, a period when people started to lose some of the freedoms they had fought for. The government believed it needed to take strict measures to safeguard the revolution, but these measures became more and more severe over time.

The Law of Suspects allowed people to be arrested based on suspicion of opposing the revolution, even with little evidence. This caused many citizens to live in fear, having to be careful about what they said and who they trusted. Now, looking back, it’s easy to see how fear started to take over liberty during this time. To me, this shows that the revolution started to move away from some of its original goals, since people could lose their freedom based on a simple accusation.

Reign of Terror

Robespierre: Platinum Challenge

Maximilien Robespierre (1758–1794) is considered one of the most influential yet controversial figures in the French Revolution. He was an attorney who supported equality and believed that the government should be responsible to common citizens. Robespierre served as a leader of the Jacobins during the time when the Reign of Terror took place. Robespierre argued that the revolution should have been protected at all costs; therefore, those who were charged as opponents of the revolution faced execution by guillotine. The number of victims at that time reached up to 17,000 people. Though Robespierre helped overthrow the monarchy and spread ideals of equality and democracy, he used extremely violent methods in achieving it. Consequently, Robespierre was arrested in 1794 and sentenced to death, thus ending the Reign of Terror.

POLITICAL, SOCIAL, AND ECONOMIC IMPACTS

THE IMPACTS

Political Impact

The revolution ended the absolute monarchy and led to a republic. It introduced important ideas like equality before the law and people having a say in government through elected representatives. However, the revolution also became very violent during the Reign of Terror, when thousands were executed. In the end, the chaos allowed Napoleon to take control as a military dictator. Even so, the revolution inspired other countries to fight for democracy and challenged the idea that kings had divine power.

Social Impact

The revolution removed the special privileges of nobles and clergy, so they no longer had automatic power over ordinary people. It promoted the idea that all men should be equal under the law. The church lost much of its influence. The Women’s March on Versailles showed women’s political power, but they still did not get equal rights, and slavery was not completely abolished in French colonies. Despite these limits, the revolution changed how people thought about social class and fairness.

Economic Impact

As a result of the revolution, feudal taxes were abolished and the land owned by nobles and the clergy was distributed among the peasants. This was the first time poor people had the opportunity to become landowners. Unfortunately, in the short term, the revolution also caused problems such as inflation and food shortages. The assignat, which was the newly issued currency in France, lost much of its value and by 1796 it had become worthless. However, Napoleon later strengthened the French economy by founding the Bank of France and controlling prices.

BIGGEST TURNING POINT

Storming of the Bastille

In my opinion, the most significant turning point of the French Revolution was the storming of the Bastille on July 14, 1789. This is when citizens finally decided to resist the monarchy's authority, defend their rights, and show the monarchs that they had enough of unfair treatment. The members of the Third Estate comprised the largest part of the French population, paid taxes, and suffered economically, but still did not have any influence on politics. The Bastille's capture showed people's willingness to change the current situation for the better. For me, this event marked a new stage of the French Revolution because it inspired people to revolt against the monarchs who had lost control of France.

SUCCESSES AND FAILURES

Successes

The French Revolution was successful in ending the absolute monarchy and reducing the power of the nobles in France. It also spread ideas like liberty, equality, and natural rights, which later influenced other countries and future democratic movements. The Declaration of the Rights of Man was one of the biggest achievements because it stated that citizens should have rights and freedoms under the law.

Failures

However, the revolution also had many failures because violence and political problems continued for years. The Reign of Terror led to the deaths of thousands of people, and many citizens still did not have equal rights, especially women. In the end, Napoleon became emperor, which showed that France moved back toward authoritarian rule instead of fully achieving democracy and equality.

Overall Judgment

I think the French Revolution achieved some of its goals, but not all of them. It ended the absolute monarchy and gave people more freedom and rights, which was one of the main goals. It also spread ideas like equality and democracy. However, the revolution became very violent during the Reign of Terror, and many innocent people died. Also, not everyone got equal rights, especially women and poor people. Overall, the revolution changed France in important ways, but it did not fully create the fair society people wanted.

LONG-TERM IMPACT QUESTION

Which impact mattered most long-term?

Of the three impacts, the political impact of the French Revolution mattered most because it permanently changed how people thought about government and authority. The revolution ended absolute monarchy and introduced the radical idea that ordinary people should have a say through elected representatives. Even though the Reign of Terror led to thousands of executions and Napoleon became a military dictator, the core political ideas survived. The revolution challenged the belief that kings had divine power and inspired other countries to fight for democracy in the decades that followed.

The social and economic changes were more limited or temporary. Nobles and clergy lost their privileges, but women still lacked equal rights and slavery continued in French colonies. Feudal taxes ended and peasants gained land, but the assignats became almost worthless, causing short-term chaos. More importantly, these social and economic changes could not have happened without the political shift first. Ending the monarchy and stripping nobles of their power made it possible to remove noble privileges and cancel feudal taxes. The political revolution gave ordinary people a voice, which then allowed them to demand fairer treatment socially and economically. Therefore, the political impact matters most because it was the foundation for everything else. Without the political revolution first, the social and economic changes would never have occurred.
